Frequently Asked Questions

Riding a bike demands a balance between thrill and responsibility. Safety is paramount—always don the necessary gear, from helmets to protective clothing. Training is key; invest time in learning proper techniques and road etiquette. Being visible to others and assuming an assertive, defensive stance on the road can save lives. Mastering braking, accelerating smoothly, and understanding your bike's maintenance needs ensures a safer ride.

Determining if your helmet needs replacement involves a few key indicators. Check for visible signs of wear, like cracks, dents, or a deteriorating outer shell; these suggest structural compromise. Inspect the inner foam for brittleness or compression, which can reduce its impact absorption ability. Consider the age of the helmet, as materials degrade over time, typically warranting replacement every five years. Any changes in fit or looseness could signify padding compression, affecting its protective function. Always replace a helmet that's been involved in a crash, regardless of visible damage, as its protective capabilities might have been compromised.

Bringing a bike out of storage requires some cautious steps to ensure it's safe for riding. Start by inspecting the overall condition—check tire pressure and condition, looking for any cracks or signs of wear. Assess the brakes to ensure they're functioning properly, and inspect the chain for rust or stiffness. Check the fluids (if applicable), like oil and brake fluid, to ensure they're at the correct levels. Verify that the battery (if your bike has one) is charged and in good condition. It's also advisable to check for any signs of pests or critters that might have taken up residence in the storage area or within the bike itself. Give the bike a thorough once-over, and if everything seems in order, take it for a slow test ride to ensure that all systems are working as they should. If there are any concerns or uncertainties, consider having a professional mechanic give it a once-over before regular use.

When riding your bike, adhering to certain rules ensures safety for yourself and others on the road. First and foremost, always wear a helmet—it's your best defense in case of an accident. Obey traffic laws just as you would in a car, including following signals, yielding to pedestrians, and using designated lanes. Stay visible by wearing bright or reflective clothing and using lights, especially in low-light conditions. Maintain a defensive mindset by anticipating the actions of other vehicles and staying alert to potential hazards. Signal your intentions clearly and communicate with other road users by using hand signals or indicators. Finally, ensure your bike is in good working condition, regularly checking brakes, tires, and lights before each ride. These rules prioritize safety and contribute to a smoother and more secure biking experience.
